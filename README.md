1.0   Brief description of the design criteria and project overview. 

A design proposal for a retaining wall at La Florida 3 will be developed. The proposed wall is shown in Figure 1 and is subjected to lateral earth pressures induced by the retained soil, as well as additional surcharge loads from a three-story building located adjacent to the retaining structure.

2.0   Sliding stability review. 

The sliding stability verification was performed using a minimum factor of safety of 1.5. The analysis was carried out by evaluating the ratio between resisting and driving forces.
The driving forces consist of the total horizontal forces (Figures 2.1 and 2.2), including the lateral earth pressure and the additional forces induced by surcharge loads.
The resisting forces are represented by the vertical forces and base friction (Figure 2.3), considering the self-weight of the retaining wall and the frictional resistance at the soil–structure interface.
Based on this analysis, a factor of safety of 2.7 was obtained, which satisfies the required design criteria.

3.0   Overturning stability review. 

The overturning stability is evaluated by comparing the stabilizing moments generated by vertical loads against the overturning moments induced by lateral earth pressures. The factor of safety is defined as the ratio of resisting moments to overturning moments, ensuring that the stabilizing effects exceed those that tend to rotate the wall about the toe.

4.0   Retaining wall design:

The design of the reinforcement steel is carried out using factored load combinations in accordance with ultimate limit state criteria. The required ultimate bending moment is determined using the following load combination:
Mu=1.2D+1.6L+1.6H
where:
•	D: represents the dead load, including the self-weight of the retaining wall and any permanent structural components, 
•	L: corresponds to the live load or surcharge applied at the ground surface, and 
•	H: denotes the lateral earth pressure acting on the wall.

4.3   Shear verification using FEM.

A simplified and conservative finite element method (FEM) approach was employed to evaluate the maximum shear stress in the retaining wall. The analysis aimed to verify that the computed stress values remain below 0.1f′c, where f′c=250 kg/cm2f'c =250kg/cm2. Compliance with this limit provides validation of the analytical model and ensures consistency with the requirements of the applicable NTC provisions, thereby contributing to the structural safety and adequate performance of the element.

5.0  Conclusions
Based on the performed analyses, the proposed retaining wall for La Florida 3 satisfies all relevant stability and structural design requirements in accordance with the applicable NTC provisions.
From a geotechnical perspective, the wall demonstrates adequate global stability. The sliding verification resulted in a factor of safety of 2.7, significantly exceeding the minimum required value of 1.5, indicating a robust resistance against horizontal displacement. Similarly, the overturning analysis yielded a factor of safety greater than 2.0, confirming that the stabilizing moments generated by the self-weight of the system effectively counteract the destabilizing effects of lateral earth pressures and surcharge loads. Additionally, the eccentricity evaluation verified that the resultant force remains within the middle third of the base, ensuring full compressive contact and preventing tensile stresses at the soil–foundation interface.
From a structural standpoint, the retaining wall was designed using factored load combinations consistent with ultimate limit state design. The flexural and shear verifications confirm that the section provides sufficient capacity to resist the applied demands. In particular, the shear capacity of the concrete was found to be adequate, eliminating the need for additional shear reinforcement. The adopted reinforcement layout was shown to be conservative, as it was governed by more critical sections within the wall.
Furthermore, the simplified finite element analysis provided an additional level of validation by confirming that the maximum shear stresses remain below the permissible limit of 0.1f′ c. This result supports both the reliability of the analytical approach and the structural integrity of the element under the considered loading conditions.
Overall, the proposed design exhibits satisfactory performance in terms of stability, strength, and serviceability. The combination of conservative assumptions, consistent analytical methods, and verification through multiple approaches ensures a safe and efficient structural solution for the retaining wall system.
